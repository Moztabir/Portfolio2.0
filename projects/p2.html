Project Overview

A math game built to feel active, engaging, and replayable

Mathive is a game-based learning project that turns math practice into a faster and more interactive experience. Instead of making students feel like they are completing ordinary worksheets, the goal is to create a system where speed, challenge, and visual feedback make learning feel more motivating.

The Problem

Math practice often feels repetitive and low-engagement

A lot of educational tools focus only on correctness, but not on excitement, pacing, or replay value. Students can lose interest when the interface feels static, the feedback is weak, or the activity feels too close to traditional homework.

  • 1

    Low motivation

    Basic drill formats can make practice feel repetitive instead of rewarding.

  • 2

    Weak feedback loops

    When players do not feel progress, urgency, or momentum, the activity becomes less memorable.

  • 3

    Missed game potential

    Many learning tools do not fully use competition, timers, scoring, and challenge systems that make games engaging.

The Solution

A game loop that makes solving math feel fast and satisfying

Mathive combines learning objectives with a more competitive game structure. Timers, quick answer cycles, score tracking, and cleaner visual feedback help turn each round into something more dynamic than ordinary practice.

“The goal was to make math practice feel less like obligation and more like momentum.”

Design Goal

Create a clean, minimal game interface that keeps the player focused on the challenge without unnecessary clutter.

Player Goal

Solve as many questions as possible, improve reaction speed, and stay engaged through challenge and progression.

Lessons Learned

What the project taught me

Mathive taught me how to balance education and entertainment in one experience. It pushed me to think about pacing, motivation, and how interface design affects how quickly players understand and respond to a challenge.

Design Learning

I learned that educational interfaces work better when the information hierarchy is immediate and the player always knows what to do next.

Development Learning

I improved at structuring game loops, handling timers and score systems, and building a more polished front-end experience.
